Similarity Transformation

Consider a non-singular matrix $T\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ and define the state variable as

$$ \tilde{x}=Tx $$

Then, the state-space model is $$ \dot{\tilde{x}}=TAT^{-1}\tilde{x}+TBu\\ y=CT^{-1}\tilde{x}+Du $$

Eigenvalue Preserving

Given a matrix $A\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ and a non-singular matrix $T\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$, define the similar matrix

$$ \tilde{A}=TAT^{-1} $$

Consider the eigenvalue operator $\lambda\{\}$, then, the following holds

$$ \lambda\{\tilde{A}\}=\lambda\{A\} $$

Transformation: diagonalization

Any matrix $A\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ with $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors can be diagonalized.

Let $v_1,v_2,\ldots,v_n$ be all eigenvectors of $A$ and $\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_n$ its respective eigenvalues. Define the matrix $T$ as $$ T=\begin{bmatrix}v_1 & v_2 & \cdots & v_n\end{bmatrix} $$ Then, a similar matrix is $$ \tilde{A}=TAT^{-1}=\Lambda=\diag(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n) $$

Modal Transformation (2x2)

If the eigenvalues are complex-conjugate in the form $$ \lambda_{1,2}=\alpha\pm j\beta $$ Then, the similar system is

$$ \dot{\tilde{x}}=\begin{bmatrix} \alpha & -\beta\\ \beta & \alpha \end{bmatrix}\tilde{x},\quad \tilde{x}(0)=Tx(0) $$

For this case, the matrix $T$ is given by $ T=\begin{bmatrix} \text{real}(v_1) & \text{imag}(v_2) \end{bmatrix} $

Modal Transformation (2x2)

If the eigenvalues are complex-conjugate in the form $$ \lambda_{1,2}=\alpha\pm j\beta $$ The matrices

$$ A=\begin{bmatrix} \alpha & -\beta\\ \beta & \alpha \end{bmatrix},\quad \text{and }\quad \tilde{A}=\begin{bmatrix} \alpha+j\beta & 0\\ 0 & \alpha-j\beta \end{bmatrix} $$

are similar!

Modal Transformation: multiples pairs

Consider $$ \lambda_{1,2}=\alpha_1\pm j\beta_1,\quad \lambda_{3,4}=\alpha_2\pm j\beta_2 $$

$$ \dot{\tilde{x}}=\begin{bmatrix} \alpha_1 & -\beta_1 & 0 & 0 \\ \beta_1 & \alpha_1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \alpha_2 & -\beta_2 \\ 0 & 0 & \beta_2 & \alpha_2 \end{bmatrix}\tilde{x} $$

Modal Transformation: multiples pairs

Consider $$ \lambda_{1,2}=\alpha_1\pm j\beta_1,\quad \lambda_{3,4}=\alpha_2\pm j\beta_2,\quad \lambda_{5,6}=\alpha_3\pm j\beta_3 $$

$$ \dot{\tilde{x}}=\begin{bmatrix} \alpha_1 & -\beta_1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \beta_1 & \alpha_1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \alpha_2 & -\beta_2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \beta_2 & \alpha_2 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \alpha_3 & -\beta_3 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \beta_3 & \alpha_3 \end{bmatrix}\tilde{x} $$

Cayley-Hamilton+Briot-Ruffini

As $\Delta(A)=0$, then

 

$$ \exp(A t)=r(A,t)=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \rho_i(t)A^i $$

Modal Transformation (2x2)

$$ \exp\Bigl(\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}\alpha & -\beta\\\beta & \alpha\end{bmatrix}}_{A} t\Bigr)=\begin{bmatrix} \rho_0(t)+\alpha \rho_1(t) & -\rho_1(t)\beta\\ \beta \rho_1(t) & \rho_0(t)+\alpha\rho_1(t) \end{bmatrix} $$

with $$ \rho_0(t)=e^{\alpha t}(\cos(\beta t)-\sin(\beta t))\\ \rho_1(t)=e^{\alpha t}\sin(\beta t) $$

Modal Transformation (2x2)

 

$$ \exp\Bigl(\begin{bmatrix}\alpha & -\beta\\\beta & \alpha\end{bmatrix} t\Bigr) \\=e^{\alpha t}\begin{bmatrix} \cos(\beta t)+(\alpha-1)\sin(\beta t) & -\beta\sin(\beta t)\\ \beta \sin(\beta t) & \cos(\beta t)+(\alpha-1)\sin(\beta t) \end{bmatrix} $$

Modal Transformation: multiples pairs

Consider $$ \lambda_{1,2}=\alpha_1\pm j\beta_1,\quad \lambda_{3,4}=\alpha_2\pm j\beta_2,\quad \lambda_{5},\lambda_6 \in \mathbb{R} $$

$$ \dot{\tilde{x}}=\begin{bmatrix} \alpha_1 & -\beta_1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \beta_1 & \alpha_1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \alpha_2 & -\beta_2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \beta_2 & \alpha_2 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \lambda_5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \lambda_6 \end{bmatrix}\tilde{x} $$

Modal Transformation: multiplicity

Consider $\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3 \in \mathbb{R}$, such that

 

$$ \Delta(s)=(s-\lambda_1)^3(s-\lambda_2)^2(s-\lambda_3) $$

 

How to put it in a state-space model?

Jordan Normal Form

$$ A=T^{-1} J T $$ or $$ J=T A T^{-1} $$

where $$ T \to \text{similarity transformation}\\ J \to \text{Jordan form} $$

Jordan Normal Form

 

For the case of real and distinct eigenvalues, the Jordan form is

$$ J=\Lambda = \diag(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_n) $$

And, $$ \exp(Jt)=\diag(e^{\lambda_1 t},e^{\lambda_2 t},\ldots,e^{\lambda_n t}) $$

Jordan Normal Form (2x2)

 

For the case of real but repetitive eigenvalues, $$ \Delta(s)=(s-\lambda_1)^2 $$ What is the Jordan form?

$$ J=? $$

Jordan Normal Form (2x2)

$$ \Delta(s)=(s-\lambda_1)^2 $$

$$ J=\begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_1 \end{bmatrix}\quad \text{(geometric multiplicity=2)} $$ or $$ J=\begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 1\\ 0 & \lambda_1 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{(geometric multiplicity=1)} $$

Algebraic and Geometric Multiplicity

Definitions:

Algebraic multiplicity = number of times the eigenvalue appears in the characteristic equation.

And

Geometric multiplicity = number of linearly independent eigenvectors associated with the eigenvalue.

$$ \text{algebraic multiplicity}\geq \text{geometric multiplicity} $$

Geometric Multiplicity

Definitions:

Geometric multiplicity ($\rho$) = number of linearly independent eigenvectors associated with the eigenvalue.

$$ \rho=\text{dim}\{\text{ker}(A-\lambda_iI)\} $$ Obs: $$ \text{ker}(M)=\{x\in\mathbb{R}^n: Mx=0\} (\text{null space}) $$

$$ J=\begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_1 & 1\\ 0 & 0 & \lambda_1 \end{bmatrix}\quad \text{(geometric multiplicity=1)} $$

Jordan Block (k x k)

 

A Jordan block of order \( k \) is a square (upper triangular) matrix of dimension \( k \), denoted \( J_k \), with its unique eigenvalue of multiplicity \( k \) on the diagonal, ones on the first subdiagonal, and zeros elsewhere.

$$ J_1=[\lambda_1],J_2=\begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 1 \\ 0 & \lambda_1 \end{bmatrix}, J_3=\begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_1 & 1\\ 0 & 0 & \lambda_1 \end{bmatrix},\ldots $$

Matrix Exponential of $J$

Consider a Jordan block of order \(2 \),

$$ J_2=\begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 1 \\ 0 & \lambda_1 \end{bmatrix} $$

What is the matrix exponential of \(J_2\)? $$ \exp(J_2 t)=? $$

Matrix Exponential of $J$

Using the Cayley-Hamilton theorem and Briot-Ruffini, we have

$$ \exp(J_2 t)=\rho_0(t)I+\rho_1(t)J_2 $$

Which results in

$$ \exp(J_2 t)=e^{\lambda_1 t}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & t\\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Matrix Exponential of $J$

Consider a Jordan block of order \(3 \),

$$ J_3=\begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_1 & 1\\ 0 & 0 & \lambda_1 \end{bmatrix} $$

What is the matrix exponential of \(J_3\)? $$ \exp(J_3 t)=? $$

Matrix Exponential of $J$

Using the Cayley-Hamilton theorem and Briot-Ruffini, we have

$$ \exp(J_3 t)=\rho_0(t)I+\rho_1(t)J_3+\rho_2(t)J_3^2 $$

Which results in

$$ \exp(J_3 t)=e^{\lambda_1 t}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & t & \frac{t^2}{2}\\ 0 & 1 & t\\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Matrix Exponential of $J$

Consider a Jordan block of order \(4 \),

$$ J_4=\begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 1 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_1 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & \lambda_1 & 1\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \lambda_1 \end{bmatrix} $$

What is the matrix exponential of \(J_4\)? $$ \exp(J_4 t)=? $$

Matrix Exponential of $J$

Using the Cayley-Hamilton theorem and Briot-Ruffini, we have

$$ \exp(J_4 t)=\rho_0(t)I+\rho_1(t)J_4+\rho_2(t)J_4^2+\rho_3(t)J_4^3 $$

Which results in

$$ \exp(J_4 t)=e^{\lambda_1 t}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & t & \frac{t^2}{2} & \frac{t^3}{3!}\\ 0 & 1 & t & \frac{t^2}{2}\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & t\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Jordan Block multiples complex pairs

Consider $$ \Delta(s)=(s-(\alpha_1\pm j\beta_1))^2 $$

$$ \begin{bmatrix} \alpha_1 +j\beta_1& 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \alpha_1-j\beta_1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \alpha_1 +j\beta_1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \alpha_1 -j\beta_1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Jordan Block: multiples complex pairs

Consider $$ \Delta(s)=(s-(\alpha_1\pm j\beta_1))^2 $$

$$ \tilde{J}_2=\begin{bmatrix} \alpha_1 & -\beta_1 & 1 & 0 \\ \beta_1 & \alpha_1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & \alpha_1 & -\beta_1 \\ 0 & 0 & \beta_1 & \alpha_1 \end{bmatrix} $$
